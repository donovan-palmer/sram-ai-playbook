## Course Overview

Human and Machine Intelligence builds upon the foundations of AIML901-MO and dives deeper into how humans and AI interact, what that means for leadership and organizations, and how to successfully lead AI adoption in organizations.

This is a real-world applications oriented course that draws on interdisciplinary insights from machine learning, cognitive science, human-computer interaction, organizational behavior, and literature to examine how and why humans and machines differ in problem-solving abilities.

By understanding the strengths and weaknesses of current and future humans and machines across a breadth of case studies, you will learn to recognize where AI can complement human intelligence and organizational processes, when human judgment is indispensable, what trade-offs emerge when employing humans versus machines, and how to design hybrid teams that outperform either humans or machines working alone.

This course is an interactive lecture-based course with reading reflection assignments and a final group project. The goal of the course is to prepare you to identify and evaluate AI opportunities, uncover human and machine blindspots, navigate ethical dilemmas, and lead teams of hybrid human and machine intelligence to effectively integrate AI into new businesses and adapt old ones.

### Final Project (55%)

Today, nearly every company represents a case for AI adoption. The real managerial question is how to do AI adoption effectively given the organization's operational processes, strategy, and culture.

In the final group project (with individual and group deliverables), you will identify, think through, and artfully write up an AI adoption playbook for a Fortune 500 company.

The goal is to produce a C-suite-ready playbook: concise, insightful, and action-oriented.

The AI Adoption Playbook should be 6 to 8 pages and structured as if being delivered to the company's executive leadership.

#### Final Project Rubric Structure

1. Executive Summary (1 page)
2. What the company does, with a focus on value generation process
3. Where AI can add value to current processes
4. How to integrate AI into current processes, including what to pilot, how to measure success, and what organizational enablers (talent, data, leadership) are required
5. What an AI-first version of this company could look like in 2031, including where significant new revenue streams could emerge

#### Milestones

- By end of Week 2: Individual 1-page outline on how the company works, focused on the problem: main revenue streams.
- By end of Week 3: Individual 1-page outline on AI adoption readiness, focused on the solution: how AI can decrease costs and increase value of new and existing revenue streams. Include business processes, specific AI tools, and rough order-of-magnitude cost/benefit numbers.
- By end of Week 4: Team interview with someone in the organization responsible for management and innovation. Submit a 1 to 3 page interview overview (one interview per team).
- By end of Week 5: Submit the final 6 to 8 page AI Adoption Playbook and present an 8-minute version to the class.

#### Final Project Evaluation Criteria

- Depth: How fully and insightfully you understand the company, industry, and AI adoption potential.
- Specificity: How concrete and detailed the analysis is, with real workflows, decisions, metrics, and implementable examples.
- Thoughtfulness: Original reasoning that acknowledges trade-offs and demonstrates managerial judgment.
- Effort: Research rigor, interview quality, source integration, and level of refinement.
- Clarity: Structure, writing quality, and professionalism of the final playbook.

Peer evaluations will be collected and used directly to help determine each individual's final project grade.

Milestone deliverables in Weeks 2 to 4 are graded on a check-plus/check/check-minus scale. The final deliverable uses a standard letter grade scale.

### Reflection Assignments (30%)

In Weeks 2, 3, and 4, you will write a short reflection paper (minimum 1 page, maximum 2 pages) on one or more assigned readings that week.

Format requirement: double-spaced, 12-point type.

#### Reflection Rubric

1. Summarize the main topic, key ideas, and management relevance in a short paragraph.
2. Explain what you find exceptional and what surprised you.
3. Describe a work/organizational experience that connects to the reading, including what matches, what does not, and what leadership questions this raises.

Reflections are graded on depth, specificity, and originality.

Each paper is graded out of 10 points:

- 1 point: summary
- 4 points: exceptional/surprising insights
- 5 points: connection to your lived managerial experience

If you use AI for light brainstorming or proofreading, that is allowed and encouraged. The thinking must still be yours.

## AIML 950 / MORS 950 Winter 2026 Schedule

### 1. Intelligence Across Scales

Guiding questions:

- How do we compare intelligence across biological organisms, artificial systems, and organizations?
- Why do size, age, and resource constraints matter for intelligence?
- What lessons about scaling intelligence can managers draw from evolution, childhood, and Moore's Law?

Readings:

- Measuring AI Ability to Complete Long Tasks (2025) by METR
- Powers of Ten by Charles and Ray Eames

### 2. Decision-Making Blindspots

Guiding questions:

- What cognitive biases systematically distort human decision-making?
- Why do AI systems introduce new kinds of blindspots?
- How can managers combine human and machine intelligence to mitigate blindspots?

Readings:

- Why People Resist Embracing AI by Julian de Freitas (2024)
- The Illusion Illusion by Tomer Ullman (2024)

### 3. Creativity

Guiding questions:

- What is creative thinking?
- How is generative AI disrupting creative work?
- How can you boost your own and your team's creativity?

Readings:

- Innovation (Chapter 5) in *Primal Intelligence* by Angus Fletcher (2026)

### 4. The Media Equation, Eliza, and AI Boyfriends

Guiding questions:

- Why do humans treat computers and AI systems as social actors, often unconsciously?
- What are the implications of anthropomorphism for trust, marketing, and organizational culture?
- How should managers responsibly design human-AI relationships in products and workplaces?

Readings:

- Chapter 1, "The Media Equation" by Byron Reeves and Cliff Nass (1996)
- Empowering AI Companions for Enhanced Relationship Marketing by Rijul Chaturvedi, Sanjeev Verma, and Vartika Srivastava (2025), with video companion

### 5. Hype Engineering and Dousing

Guiding questions:

- Why does hype emerge so predictably around new technologies like AI?
- How do managers distinguish between overpromising narratives and genuine breakthroughs?
- What strategies can leaders use to harness hype productively while avoiding disillusionment?

Readings:

- *Normal Technology* by Arvind Narayanan and Sayash Kapoor (2025)
- Chapter 1, "An Introduction to AI Hype" from *The AI Con* by Emily Bender and Alex Hanna (2025)

### 6. Recommendations

Guiding question:

- Why do people trust (or resist) recommendations differently when they come from humans versus algorithms, and what risks emerge when organizations over-rely on machine recommendations?

Readings:

- The Netflix Recommender Systems: Algorithms, Business Value, and Innovations by Carlos Gomez-Uribe and Neil Hunt (2015)

### 7. Intuition and Imagination

Guiding questions:

- What role should human intuition play in high-stakes managerial decisions alongside AI?
- How does science fiction enable leaders to build strategic visions?
- What organizational practices cultivate imagination?
- Why has common-sense reasoning been a bottleneck for AI research, and why does it matter for business?
- How do humans build common sense and what are the limits of transferring this to machines?
- What happens when organizations mistakenly assume machines have common sense?

Readings:

- Where Data-Driven Decision Making Can Go Wrong by Michael Luca and Amy Edmondson (2024)
- The Last Question by Isaac Asimov (1956)
- Optional: Common Sense by John McCarthy (2002)

### 8. Storytelling

Guiding questions:

- Why did Steve Jobs say "The most powerful person in the world is the storyteller"?
- How can you weave meaning, create serendipity, and grow wiser through story?
- How can AI make us better storytellers?

Readings:

- Why Your Intuition, Imagination, and Emotion Will Outlast AI by Eric Markowitz and Angus Fletcher (2025)
- The Secret Structure of Great Talks by Nancy Duarte (2011)

### 9. Emotions

Guiding questions:

- How well can machines recognize, respond to, and express emotion?
- How do emotions guide vision and influence?

Readings:

- Feeling Machines: Emotion AI at Affectiva by Shane Greenstein and John Masko (2019)

### 10. Presentations

- Team presentation of AI Adoption Playbook
- Format: 8-minute presentation + 2 questions from classmates
